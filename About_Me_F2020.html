Teaching Philosophy

There are a few values that I hold core when I teach my classes and I try to turn them into “ground rules” when teaching my classes. Every statistics class I teach has the following ground rules:

  • My class is a math positive space. For the 50 or 75 minutes you are in my live class, we are all math geeks who like to build each other up and tell each other how awesome we all are at math. Hearing other people say math is hard or that it’s impossible, it can make people give up. We all have some personal responsibility to how others feel in the class. There’s nothing in these classes that you would not be able to do after having an open mind, seeking help, and putting in a genuine effort. You won’t be able to do everything automatically, that isn’t the goal and it’s not a sign of weakness, it’s just a sign you need to put in some more time. It never matters where you start in class, what matters is where you end.

  • You get out of things in life what you put in. All of my classes require work. They require practice. They require learning new skills. Math is not a discipline where you listen once, do nothing, and then magically know the course content. You work hard, and I will work hard back. If you devour my practice problems, I will make you more. If you want to know more about an area of R, I will boost the content. I will always do a good job at my course, but the extra things I do for people who will appreciate it. You learn based on the effort you put in, the extra places you can go with it under my lead are a direct correlation to the effort you put in as well.

  • I will take care of all university emergencies if you learn the content. While I now get eye twitches seeing the word “uncertain”, these are uncertain times. There’s a good chance something will go wrong. There will be a tech issue of some sort. A resource might get taken down for maintenance when we need it. Trust that whatever it is that will happen, I will take care of it and make sure that you aren’t disadvantaged. We can be flexible with due dates when things go wrong, we can make alternate arrangements if tests go sideways. I have seen just about everything go wrong that could mid-semester and I have always taken care of it. If there’s an issue, politely tell me, keep working hard on the course, and I will make a game plan. Please, no angry emails about issues that are beyond my control. Trust that I am a reasonable person, I will handle reasonable requests reasonably, and give me the benefit of the doubt that I do have your best interests at heart. Also, keep in mind that I can’t fix anything I don’t know about. Please don’t sit at home completely stressed out about the assignment system, or a missing file and sit on it. Let me know. If I forgot to post a file (and it will happen, there’s 100s of files for all three of my courses), just let me know and I’ll post it. Trust is important. You trust me to fix things, and I’ll trust you to tell me when something needs fixing.

At the end of the day, I think what I teach is fun and sharing that with all of you is an honour. I hope that you will come to love it too. Best of success this semester!

-Dr. J. Tichon
